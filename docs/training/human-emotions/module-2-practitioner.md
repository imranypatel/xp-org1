## Part 1 — Multi-Stock Dynamics: When Emotional Stocks Interact

In Module 1 you mapped individual emotional stocks. In practice, stocks do not behave
independently — they interact through shared flows and linked loops.

### The Trust–Psychological Safety–Anxiety Triangle

These three stocks form the foundation of team emotional health. Changes in one reliably
affect the others:

```
Trust ──────(+)──────► Psychological Safety
  ▲                           │
  │(+)                        │(-)
  │                           ▼
Shared Success ◄──(-)──── Anxiety
  ▲                           │
  │(+)                        │(+)
  └────── Effective Action ◄──┘
             (delayed)
```

**Reading the diagram**:
- High trust increases psychological safety (people feel safe to take risks)
- High psychological safety decreases anxiety (fewer threats perceived)
- Low anxiety enables more effective action
- Effective action (delayed) produces shared success
- Shared success reinforces trust (closing the outer loop, R1)

**The coupling creates a systemic fragility**: A shock that drains trust (a broken commitment,
a public blame event) will, with a delay, increase anxiety and reduce safety. The team may
not feel the full effect for days or weeks — and by then the original cause may seem unrelated.

### Stock Interaction Exercise 2.1

1. Mark the current approximate level of each stock (1–10)
2. Identify which flow is currently strongest (which inflow or outflow dominates?)
3. Predict: if the dominant outflow continues for another month, what does the system look like?

## Part 2 — Archetypes in Emotional Systems

Systems Thinking has identified a set of recurring structural patterns — **archetypes** — that
appear across domains. Three are especially important in emotional and team dynamics.

### Archetype 1 — Escalation

**Structure** (reviewed from Module 1, now applied more deeply):

```
     R1                              R2
A's Defensive Action ──(+)──► B's Threat Perception
      ▲                                    │
      │                               (+)  │
      │                                    ▼
A's Threat Perception ◄──(+)── B's Defensive Action
```

**Recognition signals**:
- "They started it" from both parties simultaneously
- Proportionality lost — small actions trigger large reactions
- Conversations feel like position battles rather than joint inquiry
- Historical grievances resurface in every new discussion

**Common misdiagnosis**: "We need better communication." Usually wrong. The loop is structural.
Better communication without changing the threat-perception dynamic will temporarily reduce
symptoms while the structure waits for the next trigger.

**Structural interventions**:
- Introduce a circuit breaker: a cooling-off protocol or mandatory mediation window
- Create a "charitable interpretation" norm explicit enough to be enforced
- Re-frame success: make it visible that mutual de-escalation benefits both parties (change the
  incentive structure, not just the desired behaviour)
- In severe cases: introduce a trusted third party whose presence breaks the loop physically

### Archetype 2 — Shifting the Burden

**Structure**:

```
Problem Symptom ──(+)──► Symptomatic Fix (Side Effect)
      ▲                           │
      │(-)                        │(+)
      │                           ▼
      │                     Fundamental Fix ◄──(-)── Capacity/Will to Fix
      │                           │                       ▲
      └──────────────(-)──────────┘                       │
                                              Dependency ──┘
```

**In emotional systems**:

The problem symptom is team tension, conflict, or discomfort. The symptomatic fix is *avoiding
the difficult conversation*: routing around the problem, changing the subject, keeping interactions
superficial. The short-term relief from avoidance reduces the pressure to address the fundamental
fix (building trust, resolving the underlying disagreement, establishing a clear norm).

Over time:
- The team becomes dependent on avoidance as its primary emotional management strategy
- The avoided issues accumulate (frustration and resentment stocks fill)
- Capacity and will to have the fundamental conversation decrease
- The symptom periodically spikes (eruptions), triggering more avoidance

**Recognition signals**:
- "We don't talk about that" as an unspoken rule
- Topics that were once discussed now simply don't come up
- Periodic explosions of conflict followed by superficial reconciliation
- New members who raise the avoided topic are shut down quickly

**Structural interventions**:
- Make the avoidance pattern visible without blaming individuals ("I notice we tend to steer away from X — is that intentional?")
- Create a low-stakes format for raising difficult topics (anonymous retrospective inputs, written pre-reads)
- Explicitly close the issue when it is resolved — don't let it linger ambiguously
- Celebrate fundamental fixes when they happen; make the courage visible

### Archetype 3 — Tragedy of the Commons

**Structure**:

```
Individual Use of Shared Resource ──(+)──► Individual Benefit
        ▲                                           │
        │(+)                                        │
        │                              Total Use of Resource
        │                                           │(-)
        └──────(-)──── Resource Capacity ◄──────────┘
```

**In emotional systems — emotional bandwidth is the commons**:

Each team member has finite emotional bandwidth (capacity to manage complexity, absorb stress,
handle conflict constructively). When team processes or culture consistently draw from
individual emotional bandwidth faster than it replenishes, the commons collapses.

Common forms:
- One person becomes the team's emotional container (always available, always absorbing)
- On-call rotations, crisis management, and unclear boundaries drain bandwidth without refilling it
- High psychological safety in one context creates an expectation of unlimited availability

**Recognition signals**:
- "I'm exhausted from being the one who holds this team together"
- Burnout among the most engaged, most empathic team members first
- Meeting load that exceeds capacity to process between meetings
- Emotional investment expected but no structural support provided

**Structural interventions**:
- Make bandwidth visible: explicit capacity planning that includes emotional labour
- Distribute the "container" role: rotate who holds space in retrospectives and conflict
- Create structural replenishment: recovery time, celebration practices, explicit acknowledgement
- Reduce total draw: eliminate low-value meetings, clarify response-time norms

## Part 3 — Delays Cause Misdiagnosis

The most common failure mode in managing emotional dynamics is treating a delayed effect as
a new cause.

### The Attribution Mismatch

When a stock-draining event occurs (e.g., a public blame in a meeting), the full effect on
the team's trust and psychological safety stocks may not be visible for days or weeks. By
the time the behavioural symptoms appear (withdrawal, reduced participation, defensive
responses), the original event is no longer top of mind. The symptoms get attributed to
recent triggers — which are often innocent — while the true cause remains unaddressed.

**Example sequence**:

1. Week 1: Manager publicly corrects a team member in front of peers
2. Week 2: Team appears fine — carries on normally
3. Week 3: Participation in meetings drops noticeably
4. Week 4: A minor scheduling disagreement erupts into a disproportionate conflict
5. Diagnosis made: "We have a communication problem" or "personality clash between X and Y"
6. Actual root cause: Week 1 trust-draining event still reverberating through depleted stocks

**How to avoid it**:
- When symptoms appear, ask: "What happened 2–8 weeks ago that might have drained the relevant stock?"
- Map the delay explicitly in your CLD with a double-bar notation (||)
- In retrospectives, include a timeline of significant events alongside current emotional climate

### The Recovery Illusion

Because emotional recovery is also delayed, teams often make a second mistake: declaring
recovery too early. A team that had a significant trust event may appear fine after one
successful collaboration — but the stock may only be at 40%, not 100%. The next moderately
stressful event can drain it back to critical levels.

**Structural implication**: Recovery protocols need to be longer than they feel necessary.
The balancing loop (B1, Emotional Regulation) has a time constant measured in weeks, not days.

## Part 4 — The Social Mirror in Practice

The Social Mirror (B2) introduced in Module 1 has practical implications for how teams
shape their emotional culture.

### Emotional Tone Setting

The emotional tone in a team meeting is not set by a rule — it is set by the Social Mirror
loop. Whoever speaks first, whatever their emotional register, creates the initial reference
state that others calibrate against.

**Implication**: The first 3–5 minutes of a meeting or retrospective are structurally
significant. Deliberate choice of opening tone is a legitimate structural intervention —
not manipulation, but acknowledgement of how the loop works.

### Soothing vs Amplifying Responses

The Social Mirror loop can be deliberately modulated by choosing response patterns:

| If someone expresses… | Amplifying response (stay in loop) | Structural response (break loop) |
|----------------------|-------------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Frustration | "Yes, this is outrageous" | "That sounds like a blocked goal — what specifically is getting in the way?" |
| Anxiety | "You're right to be worried" | "What would reduce the uncertainty here?" |
| Blame | "I agree, they're the problem" | "What did the system make it easy or hard for them to do?" |
| Disengagement | Silence | "I notice you've been quiet — what would make this worth your time?" |

The structural response is not about suppressing emotion — it is about redirecting the loop
toward productive processing.

## Part 5 — From Diagnosis to Structural Intervention

The L2 skill shift is from *recognising* structural patterns to *designing* structural
interventions. A structural intervention changes a flow rate, a loop polarity, or a delay —
not just a person's behaviour.

### Intervention Design Framework

For any emotional dynamic you diagnose:

1. **Name the dominant loop** (is it reinforcing or balancing? Virtuous or vicious?)
2. **Identify the flow you can affect** (which inflow could you increase? Which outflow reduce?)
3. **Check for side effects** (does increasing this flow reduce another stock? Does the fix create a burden shift?)
4. **Design for the delay** (how long before the intervention takes effect? What supports the team during that period?)
5. **Make it structural, not behavioural** (the intervention must change what the system makes easy or hard — not rely on individual willpower)

### Example Intervention Design

**Situation**: Psychological safety declining in team retrospectives (people stop raising real issues)

| Step | Answer |
|------|--------|
| Dominant loop | R2 reversed — fear of consequences → silence → fewer real issues surfaced → problems fester → more fear |
| Flow to affect | Increase inflow to safety: reduce the perceived cost of speaking up |
| Side effects | Anonymous input may reduce accountability — mitigate by making actions (not speakers) the focus |
| Delay | 2–3 retrospectives before trust in new format builds |
| Structural change | Switch to anonymous pre-retro survey; team lead reads aggregated inputs; discussion focuses on systemic patterns, not individual incidents |

## Part A — Multiple Choice (10 pts each)

### Q1 — Multi-Stock Interaction

A team's trust stock is at a low level following a restructure. Team members report feeling
more anxious in meetings. Which structural relationship best explains this?

**A)** High anxiety is a cause of low trust (anxiety causes distrust)  
**B)** Low trust reduces psychological safety, which increases anxiety inflows — the stocks interact through shared loops  
**C)** These are unrelated stocks with independent dynamics  
**D)** Anxiety causes low trust only in extroverts

*Answer*: **B** — The Trust–Safety–Anxiety triangle: low trust reduces safety; reduced safety increases anxiety. The stocks interact through shared feedback paths.

---

### Q2 — Shifting the Burden Recognition

A team notices recurring interpersonal tension between two members. Rather than addressing
it directly, the team lead reshuffles work allocation so the two members rarely interact.
Tension appears to reduce. Six months later, a project requiring cross-team collaboration
fails — and the same underlying tension erupts, now involving four other team members.

This is:

**A)** Effective conflict management  
**B)** Escalation archetype  
**C)** Shifting the Burden — symptomatic fix reduces pressure to apply fundamental fix  
**D)** Tragedy of the Commons

*Answer*: **C** — Reshuffling is the symptomatic fix. The fundamental fix (resolving the structural source of tension) was never applied. The burden shifted, the avoidance dependency built, and the problem metastasised.

---

### Q3 — Tragedy of the Commons

The most empathic member of a 12-person team is known for being "always available." They
handle interpersonal conflicts, provide emotional support, and are the informal culture
keeper. Over 18 months they become visibly exhausted and eventually resign.

The structural diagnosis is:

**A)** The team lead should have supported this person more  
**B)** The team's emotional bandwidth is a shared stock; individual over-extraction depleted one contributor without structural limits  
**C)** The person lacked resilience  
**D)** The team was dysfunctional

*Answer*: **B** — Tragedy of the Commons. Emotional bandwidth is a shared resource. The team routinely drew from one person's capacity without structural replenishment or redistribution.

---

### Q4 — Delay and Attribution

A team has a difficult quarterly review where leadership publicly challenged the team's
methodology. Three months later, participation in cross-functional workshops drops
significantly. The team lead attributes this to "people being busy."

The ST diagnosis is:

**A)** The team lead is correct — workload explains participation decline  
**B)** The delay between the trust-draining event (Q review) and the behavioural symptom (withdrawal) caused misattribution  
**C)** The team members are passive-aggressive  
**D)** Quarterly reviews always cause disengagement

*Answer*: **B** — The quarterly review drained trust and safety stocks. Withdrawal appeared 3 months later (after the delay). Attribution to workload is the classic delay-induced misdiagnosis.

### Q5 — Structural Intervention Design

A team's retrospectives have become superficial. People report "everything is fine" even
when observable problems exist. You diagnose this as a reversed R1 loop (fear of
consequences → silence → problems persist).

Which is the structural intervention?

**A)** Tell team members it is safe to speak up  
**B)** Introduce anonymous written pre-retro inputs + focus on systemic patterns, not individual incidents  
**C)** Replace the team lead  
**D)** Run shorter retrospectives so people don't get tired

*Answer*: **B** — Structural change: removes the perceived cost of speaking up (changes the safety outflow rate). A and D are behavioural prescriptions that do not change the loop structure.

---

### Q6 — Social Mirror in Practice

A team lead opens every planning meeting by saying "What are we worried about?" Over six
months, planning meetings become dominated by risk discussion and rarely reach solution
design. Meeting satisfaction scores decline.

What is the structural recommendation?

**A)** Replace "What are we worried about?" with a framing that includes inquiry into strengths and solutions alongside risks  
**B)** Tell team members to be more solution-focused  
**C)** Make planning meetings shorter  
**D)** Move planning online to reduce social pressure

*Answer*: **A** — The opening question sets the Social Mirror's starting state. Changing the structural framing changes what the mirror amplifies.

### Q7 — Escalation Circuit Breaker

Two departments are in Escalation. Each interprets the other's boundary-setting as aggression.
You are asked to facilitate a resolution session.

The most structurally sound first step is:

**A)** Have each party present their grievances to the other  
**B)** Name the loop explicitly without assigning blame, then ask each party what they need to feel safe enough to de-escalate  
**C)** Determine who escalated first and address accountability  
**D)** Propose a compromise on the specific disputed issue

*Answer*: **B** — Naming the structural loop removes blame framing. Asking about safety needs targets the threat-perception stock (the actual driver) rather than the symptom (the specific dispute).

---

### Q8 — Stock-Level Awareness

A new team member joins a team that has been together for three years. In their first week,
they raise a critical process issue that the team has avoided for months. The team reacts
defensively; the new member is confused.

The ST explanation is:

**A)** The new member was too direct  
**B)** The new member's safety stock is at full because they have no history of being penalised; the team's safety stock is low because they have avoided this issue for months (Shifting the Burden pattern)  
**C)** The team is unprofessional  
**D)** New members should not raise issues in their first week

*Answer*: **B** — The new member enters without the accumulated avoidance history. The team is locked in a Shifting the Burden pattern; the new member's action threatened the symptomatic fix without the team having built capacity for the fundamental one.

## Part B — Practical Exercises

### Exercise P1 — CLD Drawing (20 pts)

Draw the Escalation archetype for the following scenario:

> Team A and Team B are working on a shared product. Team A merged a change without
> coordinating with Team B. Team B's feature broke. Team B now adds a mandatory 3-day
> review gate for all of Team A's changes. Team A, frustrated by the slowdown, starts
> routing changes through a workaround that bypasses the review. Team B discovers the
> workaround and escalates to leadership.

**Requirements**:
- Draw both reinforcing loops (R1 and R2)
- Label the loops and all causal links with polarity (+/−)
- Identify the structural circuit-breaker that would interrupt this pattern
- Annotate the delay between Team A's workaround and Team B's discovery

*Scoring*: 10 pts for correct structure, 5 pts for delay annotation, 5 pts for circuit-breaker identification.

---

### Exercise P2 — Intervention Design (20 pts)

Using the 5-step Intervention Design Framework from Module 2 Part 5, design a structural
intervention for the following situation:

> A cross-functional team holds weekly coordination calls. The calls are consistently used
> for status reporting; no one raises interdependencies or blockers until they become crises.
> The team lead suspects people are avoiding "bad news" conversations.

Complete all five steps:
1. Name the dominant loop
2. Identify the flow you can affect
3. Check for side effects
4. Design for the delay
5. Specify the structural change (not the behavioural aspiration)

*Scoring*: 4 pts per step.

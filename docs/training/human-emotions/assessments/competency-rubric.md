# Competency Rubric — Mastering Human Emotions through Systems Thinking

**Program**: Mastering Human Emotions through Systems Thinking  
**Version**: v0.8.0  
**Issue**: [#27](https://github.com/imranypatel/xp-org1/issues/27)  
**Used by**: Team leads for L1, L2, and L3 real-world assessment

---

## Overview

This rubric defines observable behaviours at each competency level. Team leads use it to
assess real-world performance — in discussions, retrospectives, issue analyses, and team
interactions — not just quiz scores. The rubric translates abstract ST concepts into
concrete, verifiable professional behaviour.

---

## Dimension 1 — Identifying Emotional Stocks and Flows

| Level | Observable Behaviour |
|-------|---------------------|
| **L1 — Emerging** | Describes emotions as events ("she was angry," "the meeting was tense"). Does not differentiate between triggers and stocks. Attributes emotional states to personalities. |
| **L2 — Developing** | Recognises that emotional states persist over time. Can name trust, anxiety, and psychological safety as stocks. Occasionally confuses triggers with flows. |
| **L3 — Proficient** | Consistently uses stock language ("trust has been draining since…"). Names inflows and outflows for a given situation. Distinguishes a trigger (event) from a flow rate (structural driver). |
| **L4 — Mastery** | Spontaneously maps emotional stocks in discussions and retrospectives. Identifies which stocks are currently most depleted and directs attention and intervention toward those. |

---

## Dimension 2 — Feedback Loop Recognition

| Level | Observable Behaviour |
|-------|---------------------|
| **L1 — Emerging** | Does not use loop language. Describes situations as linear cause-and-effect chains. Cannot distinguish reinforcing from balancing loops. |
| **L2 — Developing** | Can identify that a dynamic "keeps going in the same direction" (reinforcing) or "tries to reach balance" (balancing) when prompted. May confuse loop polarity. |
| **L3 — Proficient** | Correctly identifies the dominant loop type in a scenario without prompting. Labels loop polarity accurately. Recognises when a virtuous loop has reversed to vicious. |
| **L4 — Mastery** | Draws multi-loop CLDs in real discussions. Explains which loop is currently dominant and why. Identifies interaction effects between two simultaneous loops. |

---

## Dimension 3 — Archetype Application

| Level | Observable Behaviour |
|-------|---------------------|
| **L1 — Emerging** | No familiarity with archetypes. Diagnoses team problems with individual-level explanations ("personality clash," "bad attitude"). |
| **L2 — Developing** | Can name Escalation, Shifting the Burden, and Tragedy of the Commons when given a description. May struggle to identify them in ambiguous real situations. |
| **L3 — Proficient** | Identifies archetypes in real team situations without being prompted. Names the structural driver rather than the symptom. Proposes structural (not behavioural) interventions aligned to the archetype. |
| **L4 — Mastery** | Detects archetypes early in their development (before they are fully entrenched). Designs structural interventions that address the archetype at the leverage level rather than symptom. Coaches others to see the archetype. |

---

## Dimension 4 — Delay Awareness

| Level | Observable Behaviour |
|-------|---------------------|
| **L1 — Emerging** | Expects immediate effects from actions ("I apologised — it should be fine now"). Misattributes delayed effects to new causes. |
| **L2 — Developing** | Recognises that trust recovery takes time. May underestimate delay length. |
| **L3 — Proficient** | Explicitly accounts for delays when designing interventions ("this will take 3–4 weeks to show in behaviour"). Does not declare recovery before the regulation loop has had time to close. Annotates delays in CLDs. |
| **L4 — Mastery** | Uses delay awareness to prevent misdiagnosis in real events. Designs monitoring signals with delay in mind ("we will check participation rates in the third retrospective, not the first"). |

---

## Dimension 5 — Systemic Intervention Design

| Level | Observable Behaviour |
|-------|---------------------|
| **L1 — Emerging** | Proposes behavioural prescriptions ("tell them to be nicer," "remind people to be more open"). Does not consider structural change. |
| **L2 — Developing** | Proposes process changes but does not explicitly link them to a loop or stock. Interventions are structural in form but not explained systemically. |
| **L3 — Proficient** | Designs interventions that change a flow rate or loop polarity. Names the Meadows level of the intervention. Considers at least one side effect. Distinguishes structural fixes from symptomatic fixes. |
| **L4 — Mastery** | Selects interventions at the highest feasible Meadows level with explicit reasoning. Designs for side effects proactively. Includes monitoring signals in the intervention design. Evaluates multiple options before committing. |

---

## Level Thresholds for Progression

| Training Level | Required Rubric Rating | On Which Dimensions |
|---------------|----------------------|---------------------|
| **L1 pass** | Developing on all 5 | Dimensions 1–5 |
| **L2 pass** | Proficient on Dimensions 1, 2, 3 + Developing on 4, 5 | Assessed via quiz and Exercise 2.2 |
| **L3 pass** | Proficient on all 5 dimensions for ≥2 of 4 portfolio items | Assessed via portfolio review |

## Using This Rubric in Practice

Team leads assess team members across **all interactions**, not just quiz submissions.
Evidence for rubric ratings may come from:

- Contributions to GitHub issue analyses (how a member describes team dynamics)
- Behaviour in retrospectives (do they name archetypes? propose structural changes?)
- 1:1 conversations about team challenges
- How they frame situations in written communications (Slack, issue comments, PR reviews)

Update the [Progress Tracker](./progress-tracker.md) when a team member demonstrates a
level change on any dimension.

## Part 1 — From Practitioner to Systems Leader

The shift from L2 to L3 is not about learning more concepts — it is a shift in *stance*.

An L2 practitioner applies the framework to situations: recognises the archetype, names the
loop, proposes the intervention. This is valuable. An L3 systems leader does something
different: they *change the system's capacity to understand itself*.

The L3 practitioner builds the team's ability to use the framework independently. They
design structures (norms, processes, rituals) that embed ST awareness into how the team
operates. They create conditions under which the team can self-correct without needing an
outside diagnosis.

> **The L3 question is not "What is the dynamic here?" It is "How do I design this team so
> that it can answer that question itself?"**

## Part 2 — Psychological Safety as a Managed Stock

At L3, psychological safety is not a hoped-for atmosphere — it is a stock with known inflows,
outflows, and structural levers. Your role as a systems leader is to manage it actively.

### The Four Stages of Safety (Timothy Clark, 2020)

Clark identifies four sequential stages of psychological safety, each of which depends on
having built the previous one:

| Stage | Description | Stock prerequisite |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| Inclusion Safety | I feel safe to be who I am in this group | Baseline trust |
| Learner Safety | I feel safe to ask questions, make mistakes, and try | Inclusion safety |
| Contributor Safety | I feel safe to add value and challenge the status quo | Learner safety |
| Challenger Safety | I feel safe to challenge authority, norms, and direction | All prior stages |

**Systems insight**: These stages are sequential stocks. You cannot jump from Inclusion to
Challenger. Building Challenger Safety before Learner Safety is structurally unstable —
the team will not sustain it.

**Implication for systems leadership**: When a team has low Challenger Safety, the default
assumption is "people are too timid." The systemic diagnosis is "Learner Safety has not been
adequately built — challenges feel too risky because the cost of being wrong is too high."
The fix is structural: increase the inflows to Learner Safety first.

### Structural Levers for Psychological Safety

| Lever | Mechanic | Loop affected |
|-------|----------|--------------|
| Blameless Post-Mortems | Decouples analysis from punishment; separates the system from the person | Reduces outflow from Learner Safety |
| Appreciation Practices | Makes positive contributions visible and recognised | Increases inflow to Trust and Enthusiasm |
| Explicit Norms | Reduces ambiguity about what is safe to say | Reduces Anxiety inflow (clarity reduces threat) |
| Designated Devil's Advocate | Makes dissent a role, not a personality trait | Reduces cost of speaking against consensus |
| Response Protocols | Pre-agreed responses to disagreement or failure | Removes unpredictability from the Safety outflow |

## Part 3 — Facilitation as Systems Intervention

Every facilitated session — a retrospective, a planning meeting, a conflict resolution
conversation — is a structural intervention in the team's emotional system.

### The Blameless Retrospective (ST Framing)

A blameless retrospective is not about avoiding accountability. It is about correctly
attributing cause: to the system, not the individual. The premise — drawn directly from
ST — is that systems produce the behaviour they are designed to produce. When a failure
occurs, the question is "what did the system make easy?" not "who chose wrongly?"

**Facilitation structure**:

**Opening (5 min)**: Explicitly name the loop you are building.
> "We are here to improve the system. Every observation we make today is information about
> the system's structure — not an evaluation of any individual's character or competence."

**Data gathering (15 min)**: Anonymous written inputs on three questions:
1. What did the system make hard to do well?
2. What did the system make easy to do poorly?
3. What one structural change would most improve outcomes?

**Pattern finding (15 min)**: Group inputs by theme (not by person). Name the dominant loops.
> "These six inputs describe the same dynamic: when [X] happens, [Y] becomes harder, which
> leads to [Z]. This looks like a Shifting the Burden pattern."

**Structural intervention design (10 min)**: For the top 1–2 patterns, design an intervention
using the L2 framework. Ask: what flow can we change? What norm? What process?

**Commitment (5 min)**: One structural change. Named owner. Explicit timeline. Review at next retro.

**What to avoid**:
- "We should communicate better" (behavioural prescription, not structural change)
- Agreement without action (closes the B1 loop artificially without real stock change)
- Majority vote on the "real problem" (social pressure distorts diagnosis)

### Facilitating Conflict with the Escalation Archetype

When facilitating a conflict between two parties, the Escalation archetype diagnosis
reframes the conversation structurally:

1. **Name the loop without blame**: "What I'm hearing is that both of you feel the other's
   actions have been threatening. That's the structure of escalation — the loop amplifies
   regardless of who 'started it.'"

2. **Break the symmetry**: "Let's step out of the loop for a moment. What does each of you
   actually need from the other to feel safe enough to collaborate?"

3. **Design a circuit breaker**: Agree on a protocol for when either party feels the loop
   re-engaging (a pause signal, a named mediator, a written process).

4. **Make de-escalation visible**: Explicitly acknowledge when one party takes a de-escalating
   action. Make the virtuous loop as visible as the vicious one was.

## Part 4 — Coaching Through Systemic Reframing

One of the most high-leverage L3 skills is helping others reframe emotional reactions as
system signals. This is not about dismissing emotion — it is about giving it useful meaning.

### The Reframing Sequence

When a team member comes to you in an emotional state:

1. **Receive the signal** — Do not rush to reframe. The person must feel heard before they
   can engage analytically. Use presence, not problem-solving.

2. **Name the stock** — Together, name what is actually depleted or filled.
   > "It sounds like trust took a significant hit in that meeting. Is that what's driving the
   > strength of what you're feeling?"

3. **Identify the flow** — "What drained it? What was the specific event, and what did it
   make you feel was no longer reliable?"

4. **Look for the loop** — "Is this the first time this has happened, or does it follow a pattern?
   What do you do when this happens — and does that help the situation or make it worse?"

5. **Find the leverage** — "If you could change one structural thing — a process, a norm, a
   communication protocol — what would have the most impact on this pattern?"

6. **Protect the regulation delay** — "What do you need in the next 48 hours to let the
   regulation loop do its work before you take action?"

### What This Is Not

Systemic reframing is not:
- Intellectualising to avoid feeling ("let's not get emotional about this")
- Explaining away impact ("it's just the structure, no one intended it")
- Using ST vocabulary to take the agency away from the person

It is: giving the person a more useful model that connects their emotional experience to
structural causes — which opens up structural options that pure individual-level
interpretation does not.

## Part 5 — Organisational Emotional Culture as a System

At the organisational level, emotional culture is not the sum of individual emotional
states — it is an emergent property of the structural loops the organisation has designed
(deliberately or by accident).

### The Culture Archetype: Success to the Successful

In many organisations, emotional resources (recognition, support, interesting work, access
to leadership) flow disproportionately to those already in good standing. This is the
"Success to the Successful" archetype:

```
High-Trust Member ──(+)──► More Collaboration Opportunities
       ▲                            │
       │(+)                         │(+)
       │                    More Shared Success
       │                            │(+)
       └──────── Increased Trust ◄──┘

Low-Trust Member ──(+)──► Fewer Opportunities
       ▲                        │
       │(-)                     │(-)
       │                 Fewer Successes
       │                        │(-)
       └──── Continued Low Trust ◄┘
```

The structural result: high-trust members' stocks compound. Low-trust members' stocks
deplete. This is not caused by bad intentions — it is caused by a structure that routes
resources toward established success. The fix is structural: deliberate allocation of
collaboration opportunities to those with lower trust stocks; explicit visibility of
emerging contributors.

### Leverage Points for Organisational Emotional Culture

| Meadows Level | Emotional Culture Application |
|---------------|-------------------------------|
| L9 (Numbers) | Tracking metrics that make emotional stocks visible (retention, participation rates, survey scores) |
| L7 (Feedback loops) | Introducing feedback mechanisms: regular pulse surveys, skip-level conversations, anonymous channels |
| L5 (Rules) | Explicit norms: blameless retrospectives, appreciation practices, response protocols |
| L4 (Self-organisation) | Capability to redesign its own emotional norms without external intervention |
| L2 (Paradigm) | Shift from "emotions are personal, not professional" to "emotions are system signals" |

## Part 6 — The Emotional ST Full Analysis Framework

For any significant event, issue, or team dynamic, an L3 practitioner can apply the full
framework:

```
1. STOCKS      — What emotional stocks are involved? What are their current levels?
2. FLOWS       — What inflows and outflows are active? What is driving them?
3. LOOPS       — What reinforcing and balancing loops are operating?
                 Are they running virtuous or vicious?
4. DELAYS      — Where are the significant delays? What misattributions might they cause?
5. ARCHETYPES  — Which archetype (if any) best describes the structure?
6. LEVERAGE    — At which Meadows level is the most effective intervention possible?
7. SIDE EFFECTS — What might the intervention drain or fill unintentionally?
8. MONITORING  — How will you know if the intervention is working?
                 What stock level or flow rate is your signal?
```

## Portfolio Requirements

To pass L3, submit a portfolio demonstrating application of the full framework in real contexts.

### Required Evidence Items

| Item | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Evidence 1 | Written analysis of a real team event using the full 8-step framework above. Must include a CLD with at least two loops. |
| Evidence 2 | A second analysis from a different domain (e.g., if Evidence 1 is from a planning meeting, Evidence 2 should be from a different context — a conflict, a training session, an operations review) |

### Assessment Criteria

Each evidence item is evaluated against the competency rubric:
- Structural diagnosis (not individual blame)
- Correct loop identification (loop type and polarity)
- Leverage point at appropriate Meadows level
- Account for delays
- Proposed intervention is structural (not behavioural prescription)

**Passing standard**: L3 pass requires Proficient or Mastery rating on all five dimensions
for at least two of the four evidence items.

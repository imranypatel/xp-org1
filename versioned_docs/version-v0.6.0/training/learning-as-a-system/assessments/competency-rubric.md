# Competency Rubric — Learning as a System

**Program**: Learning as a System (LaaS)  
**Use**: Team leads use this rubric to assess observable behavior in daily work — not just quiz scores.  
**Levels**: L1 (Introduction) · L2 (Practitioner) · L3 (Mastery)  
**Related**: [ST Foundations Competency Rubric](../../systems-thinking/assessments/competency-rubric.md)

---

## How to Use This Rubric

This rubric describes **observable behaviors** — what you can actually see in meetings, documents, code reviews, and conversations. It complements quiz scores by anchoring progression to real-world practice.

To assess a team member:
1. Observe their behavior across at least 2–3 relevant situations
2. Find the level that best describes their **typical** behavior (not their best day)
3. Use the rubric to identify the gap between their current level and the next

A team member may be L2 in one domain and L1 in another. That is expected and normal.

## Domain 1: Personal Learning Management

*How a team member manages their own learning system — stocks, flows, and leverage points in their personal development.*

| Criterion | L1 — Introduction | L2 — Practitioner | L3 — Mastery |
|-----------|------------------|--------------------|--------------|
| **Learning goal setting** | Sets learning goals based on desire or manager suggestion; goals are vague or output-based ("read the documentation") | Sets structured learning goals tied to specific competency gaps; distinguishes knowledge stock from skill stock goals | Sets portfolio learning goals with explicit structural design: names the inflows, identifies forgetting outflows to reduce, tracks stock accumulation over time |
| **Practice approach** | Studies in long single sessions; relies on re-reading; does not distinguish knowledge from skill; surprised when skills don't translate | Uses spaced practice and retrieval practice consciously; balances theory inflows with application practice; monitors forgetting | Designs deliberate practice cycles; adjusts interval schedules based on observed outflow rate; uses teaching/explaining as a re-inflow mechanism; visible progress tracking |
| **Handling plateaus** | Frustrated by plateaus; increases hours without structural change; may give up | Recognizes plateaus as stock-flow dynamics; diagnoses whether the constraint is effort, inflow quality, or outflow rate | Diagnoses plateau archetype (Limits to Growth vs. Fixes that Fail); identifies the binding constraint; redesigns the learning structure rather than increasing effort |
| **Failure response** | Self-criticism or avoidance after failure; does not connect failure to structural causes | Identifies which stock was insufficient (knowledge vs. skill vs. confidence); adjusts approach rather than effort alone | Models the vicious R1 loop activation explicitly; designs confidence restoration strategy; returns the loop to virtuous direction within days |

## Domain 2: Issue Writing and Problem Diagnosis

*How a team member applies LaaS ST vocabulary in GitHub issues and ST analysis sections.*

| Criterion | L1 — Introduction | L2 — Practitioner | L3 — Mastery |
|-----------|------------------|--------------------|--------------|
| **Learning failure diagnosis** | Describes symptoms ("team doesn't know X"); may attribute to motivation or individual effort | Identifies affected stocks, names the outflow that caused degradation or the inflow that is absent; names one loop | Complete ST analysis: stocks, flows, loops, delay, archetype, Meadows leverage point; structural fix that addresses root cause |
| **Archetype recognition** | Not applied | Identifies one archetype (e.g., Shifting the Burden, Eroding Goals) by name with basic justification | Identifies stacked or interacting archetypes; names the archetype in the team/organizational learning context; proposes leverage-point intervention |
| **Vocabulary precision** | Uses ST vocabulary inconsistently; occasionally confuses stocks and flows | Uses stocks, flows, loops, delays correctly; occasionally mistakes archetypes | Consistently correct vocabulary; applies Argyris (single/double loop) and Senge (5 disciplines) when relevant; cites Meadows level appropriately |

## Domain 3: Meetings and Team Discussions

*How a team member contributes to learning-related discussions in meetings, retrospectives, and planning.*

| Criterion | L1 — Introduction | L2 — Practitioner | L3 — Mastery |
|-----------|------------------|--------------------|--------------|
| **Diagnosing recurring patterns** | Notes that the same problem recurs; attributes to bad luck or individual failure | Asks "what structural condition is producing this?" in real-time; names a loop or delay that explains recurrence | Draws a quick verbal CLD of the pattern; proposes which leverage point to address; invites the team to map the system collaboratively |
| **Learning culture contribution** | Participates in team learning when prompted; shares knowledge when asked | Proactively shares what they learned; documents new knowledge in team wiki; acknowledges their own gaps publicly | Actively reinforces the learning culture R1 loop; celebrates team members' sharing; documents retrospective insights; models psychological safety by sharing own mistakes |
| **Training advocacy** | Completes training when required; no visible advocacy | Recommends specific training approaches to colleagues based on LaaS principles | Designs peer learning sessions; facilitates team learning retrospectives; proposes structural changes to how the team learns (not just more training hours) |

## Domain 4: Organizational Learning Design

*How a team member contributes to organizational learning system design — applicable to team leads, senior members, and L3 practitioners.*

| Criterion | L1 — Introduction | L2 — Practitioner | L3 — Mastery |
|-----------|------------------|--------------------|--------------|
| **Knowledge stock awareness** | Aware that team members have different knowledge levels | Identifies concentration risks (bus factor) and weak documentation flows; proposes documentation | Maps the organizational knowledge stock explicitly: IK (individual), DK (documented), EM (embedded in process); identifies vulnerable stocks |
| **Structural intervention design** | Recommends more training or documentation when gaps appear | Designs specific inflow enhancements: pairing programs, knowledge base contribution, structured mentoring | Designs multi-lever interventions: strengthens inflows, reduces outflow (attrition via culture), reduces concentration risk, and addresses psychological safety as structural lynchpin |
| **Learning ROI framing** | Frames training in terms of hours spent | Frames training in terms of competency stock growth; acknowledges the delay before benefits appear | Explains the delay and stock dynamics to leadership to set realistic expectations; frames training investment as a structural change to inflows, not a one-time event |

## Domain 5: Mentoring and Coaching

*How a team member helps others apply LaaS concepts — relevant from L2 onward.*

| Criterion | L1 — Introduction | L2 — Practitioner | L3 — Mastery |
|-----------|------------------|--------------------|--------------|
| **Teaching approach** | Explains facts and concepts when asked | Helps colleagues identify their own structural insight rather than providing the answer; uses questions to surface the loop | Coaches colleagues through a full LaaS diagnosis; uses the competency rubric to guide the conversation; is comfortable not having the answer |
| **Modeling** | Applies LaaS to their own work | Visible in documenting their learning; shares their own failures and structural explanations publicly | Explicitly narrates their learning system design choices: "I'm using spaced retrieval rather than re-reading because the forgetting outflow is my binding constraint" |
| **Peer assessment** | Not applicable at L1 | Can provide formative feedback on Module 1 quiz and CLD exercises | Certified to assess L1 and L2 practitioners using this rubric; counter-signs L3 portfolio submissions |

## Level Summary

| Level | Descriptor | Key Markers |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **L1 — Introduction** | Aware that learning is a system; can name stocks, flows, and the R1/B1 loops; applies concepts to their own learning | Vocabulary present; personal learning improved; quizzes pass threshold |
| **L2 — Practitioner** | Diagnoses learning archetypes; designs structural interventions for self and team; produces CLDs and ST analyses | Practical exercises submitted; real issue analyses present; visible in team discussions |
| **L3 — Mastery** | Models organizational learning system; facilitates team retrospectives; coaches others; designs structural knowledge transfer programs | Full portfolio complete; team lead counter-signed; facilitation and coaching evidence present |

---

## Relationship to ST Foundations Rubric

LaaS competency builds on ST Foundations competency. A team member should be at least ST Foundations L1 to be LaaS L1, and ideally ST Foundations L2 to be LaaS L2. The two rubrics are complementary:

- **ST Foundations rubric** → general ST vocabulary and cross-domain application
- **LaaS rubric** → ST applied specifically to the learning domain (personal and organizational)

When assessing a team member, use both rubrics. ST Foundations L3 + LaaS L3 is the full dual-certification path.

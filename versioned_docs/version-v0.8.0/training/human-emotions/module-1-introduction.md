## Part 1 — Emotions Are Not Events

### The Linear Model We Were Taught

Most people carry an implicit model of emotions that looks like this:

```
Stimulus → Emotion → Response
```

A colleague criticises your idea → you feel embarrassed → you go quiet. Simple. Linear. Causal.

This model is not wrong — it captures a moment. But it misses almost everything that matters:
why the same criticism produces embarrassment in one person and curiosity in another; why a
team that handled conflict well for months suddenly cannot; why a single apology sometimes
repairs trust instantly and sometimes changes nothing at all.

The linear model treats emotions as *events*. They appear, they pass, they leave no structural
residue. Systems Thinking reveals that this is fundamentally incorrect.

### Emotions Are Stocks

A **stock** is anything that accumulates and drains over time. It is a level, not an event.
Trust is a stock. Psychological safety is a stock. Anxiety is a stock. Each of these fills
and empties according to flows — and the current level of the stock determines how much
capacity the system has to absorb the next event.

> **Key insight**: You cannot understand a person's emotional reaction by looking at the
> trigger. You have to know the current level of the stock. A person with low trust in a team
> will react to an ambiguous message very differently from someone whose trust stock is full.

### The Five Core Emotional Stocks

| Stock | Definition | Range |
|-------|------------|-------|
| **Trust** | Accumulated confidence that others act in good faith | Low: suspicion ↔ High: secure reliance |
| **Psychological Safety** | Accumulated belief that it is safe to speak, question, and fail in this context | Low: threat ↔ High: safety |
| **Anxiety** | Accumulated sense of unresolved uncertainty or threat | Low: calm ↔ High: overwhelmed |
| **Enthusiasm** | Accumulated energy and desire to engage | Low: disengagement ↔ High: motivated investment |
| **Frustration** | Accumulated response to blocked goals | Low: acceptance ↔ High: resentment |

These five stocks interact. A high Anxiety level drains Enthusiasm. Low Trust drains Psychological
Safety. High Psychological Safety replenishes Enthusiasm. You cannot treat one stock in isolation.

## Part 2 — Flows: What Fills and What Drains

Every stock has **inflows** (rates of accumulation) and **outflows** (rates of drain).
Understanding the flows is understanding the causes — not the triggers, but the structural
drivers.

### Trust: The Central Stock

```
INFLOWS (Trust filling):          OUTFLOWS (Trust draining):
─────────────────────────         ────────────────────────────
Commitments kept                  Commitments broken
Transparent communication         Perceived hidden agendas
Recognition of contribution       Credit taken without acknowledgement
Mistakes handled with curiosity   Blame and public criticism
Consistency of behaviour          Inconsistent or unpredictable actions
Expressed care for the person     Being treated as a resource, not a person
```

### Anxiety: The Risk Stock

```
INFLOWS (Anxiety filling):        OUTFLOWS (Anxiety draining):
────────────────────────          ─────────────────────────────
Role ambiguity                    Clear expectations and scope
Excessive workload                Workload matched to capacity
Unpredictable environment         Reliable processes and communication
Perceived lack of control         Autonomy and influence over outcomes
Unresolved interpersonal tension  Addressed and closed conflicts
```

### Psychological Safety: The Foundation Stock

Psychological safety is not about being "nice". It is Amy Edmondson's (1999) empirically
validated construct: the team belief that one will not be punished for speaking up, asking
questions, admitting mistakes, or raising concerns. It is the bedrock stock that enables
all learning and collaboration.

```
INFLOWS (Safety filling):         OUTFLOWS (Safety draining):
──────────────────────────        ────────────────────────────
Mistakes treated as learning      Blame after failures
Dissent welcomed                  Silence after speaking up punished
Curiosity questions rewarded      Questions treated as incompetence
Inclusive participation           Dominant voices silencing others
```

## Part 3 — Feedback Loops: How Emotions Self-Amplify and Self-Correct

Stocks do not simply fill and drain in response to external events. They trigger *flows* through
feedback loops — and those loops determine whether an emotional dynamic stabilises, escalates,
or spirals.

### The Trust Spiral — Reinforcing Loop (R1)

```
Trust ──(+)──► Openness ──(+)──► Collaboration
  ▲                                      │
  └──────(+)──── Success ◄──(+)──────────┘
```

**How it works**: Higher trust enables more openness. Greater openness enables genuine
collaboration. Effective collaboration produces shared successes. Shared successes reinforce
trust. This is a *virtuous* reinforcing loop.

**The same loop reversed becomes vicious**:

```
Low Trust ──(+)──► Guardedness ──(+)──► Surface Compliance
    ▲                                         │
    └──────(+)──── Failure/Stagnation ◄───────┘
```

Low trust → defensiveness → surface compliance (not real collaboration) → poor outcomes →
confirmation that others cannot be trusted. The loop accelerates in the direction it starts.

**Implication**: The first task in any new team or troubled team is to identify whether the
trust loop is running virtuous or vicious — and to find the structural intervention that
can reverse it (not the motivational speech, but the structural change).

### The Emotional Regulation Loop — Balancing Loop (B1)

```
Desired Emotional State ──(+)──► Gap ──(+)──► Regulation Effort
                                               │
Actual Emotional State ◄────────(-)───────────┘
```

**How it works**: There is a gap between how you want to feel and how you actually feel.
That gap drives regulation behaviour (resting, reframing, seeking support, physical activity).
The regulation effort reduces the gap by either changing the actual state or adjusting
expectations of the desired state.

**The delay matters**: Regulation takes time. If team members are driven by urgency to
"get over it" before the regulation loop has closed, the stock remains depleted — and the
next event hits a partially empty stack.

### The Social Mirror — Balancing Loop (B2)

```
Expressed Emotion ──(+)──► Social Response ──(+)──► Adjustment of Expression
         ▲                                                    │
         └──────────────────────────────────────(-)───────────┘
```

How we express emotion is modulated by how others respond. If expressing concern is met
with dismissal, expression is suppressed. If expressing frustration is met with curiosity,
expression becomes more precise. The social environment shapes emotional communication
dynamically.

## Part 4 — Delays: The Source of Misdiagnosis

In emotional systems, the most common diagnostic error is ignoring delays.

### Trust Asymmetry Delay

Trust builds slowly (months of consistent behaviour) and drains rapidly (one significant
betrayal). This asymmetry means:

- Damage from a single event can exceed months of investment
- Recovery requires more investment than was present before the damage
- Teams that have experienced a trust collapse often return to conflict before they have
  fully recovered — not because they are fragile, but because the stock has not refilled

### Emotional Afterburn

After a difficult meeting or conflict, the emotional stocks of the participants do not reset
to zero when the meeting ends. Anxiety, frustration, and reduced trust persist. The next
interaction happens with these depleted stocks as the starting condition — even if hours
or days have passed and the conversation is ostensibly about something unrelated.

This is why "that was weeks ago, why are we still tense?" — the delay between the
stock-draining event and the stock-refilling time is longer than people expect.

## Part 5 — A First Archetype: Escalation

The **Escalation** archetype is perhaps the most recognisable in emotional systems.

```
Person A's Threat Perception ──(+)──► Person A's Defensive Action
            ▲                                    │
            │(+)                             (+)│
Person B's Defensive Action ◄────── Person B's Threat Perception
```

Each person's defensive action is perceived by the other as a threat. The threat increases
defensive action. The defensive action increases the other's threat perception. This is a
double-reinforcing loop — two linked vicious cycles feeding each other.

**The structural insight**: No amount of telling two people to "calm down" or "be more
professional" will break this loop while the structural conditions (mutual threat perception
and no circuit-breaker mechanism) remain intact. The fix is structural: a trusted mediator,
a cooling-off protocol, or a shared explicit commitment to interpret ambiguity charitably.

## Key Vocabulary (L1)

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Emotional stock | An emotion that accumulates and drains over time; a level, not an event |
| Trigger | An event that affects a flow into or out of an emotional stock |
| Inflow | A rate that increases an emotional stock |
| Outflow | A rate that decreases an emotional stock |
| Reinforcing loop | A feedback loop in which change propagates in the same direction (virtuous or vicious) |
| Balancing loop | A feedback loop that drives toward a target or equilibrium |
| Delay | A lag between a cause and its effect; source of oscillation and misdiagnosis |
| Escalation archetype | Two linked reinforcing loops in which each party's defensive response intensifies the other's threat perception |
